Backup and transfer

Sealed copy

A sealed copy holds both records and stays unreadable without the passphrase. This is the safe way to keep a backup or move to a new phone.

Paper log

A sheet to hand to a clinician, printed at true size so the cycle chart can be measured with a ruler. Paper cannot be reached from anywhere else, and nothing on it identifies you unless you put a name on it.

Bring a record in

Reads an export from another tracker or a plain copy from Ephemeris. It handles JSON (Clue and Flo both export JSON), a plain table, and an Apple Health export. Nothing is sent anywhere: the file is read here, on this device.

Clue sends a password protected zip. Unzip it first and pick the file inside. Neither Clue nor Flo records where your cycles started, so starts are worked out from the bleeding days themselves. Everything brought in is marked as recalled.

A plain copy is readable by anything that touches it, including whatever backs up your downloads folder. Once it leaves this app it is out of reach.

Measures that destroy things

The settings behind this page can permanently erase this record. They exist for someone who expects a device to be taken, and they are dangerous to switch on casually.

  • They cannot be undone. Erased means gone, with no recovery of any kind.
  • They do not defeat a forensic examination of the raw storage. They defeat a person holding your phone and demanding you open it.
  • Keep a sealed copy somewhere else before you turn any of this on.

Keeping the other record believable

A record that has not been written to for months is a tell. With this on, the second record quietly gains ordinary looking entries whenever you open this one.

A wipe passphrase destroys this record the moment it is entered. It reports the same message as a wrong passphrase, so nothing on screen shows what happened. Enter your second passphrase afterwards to show an ordinary record.

Field Instrument FI-117

Ephemeris

An ephemeris is a table of predicted positions worked out from earlier observation. Never certain, always improving with the record behind it.

What this instrument does with your record

  • It runs from the file itself. There is no account, no server, and no company holding anything.
  • It cannot reach the network. The page forbids every outbound connection, so there is nothing to intercept and nothing to sell.
  • What sits on this device is ciphertext. Symptom names, dates, and notes never touch storage in readable form.
  • The passphrase is put through Argon2id, which is built to be expensive in memory as well as time, so guessing it with a rack of graphics cards is slow and costly.
  • Both records are padded to the same fixed size, so the file does not reveal how much is written in either one.

What it cannot do

  • It cannot protect you from a device that is already compromised. Anything watching the screen or the keyboard sees what you see.
  • A second passphrase answers a demand to unlock. It does not defeat a forensic examination of the raw storage.
  • Deleted data can survive in flash memory below the level any web page can reach.
  • Screenshots, backups you make yourself, and plain copies you export are outside its control.
  • Predictions are arithmetic on your own history. They are not medical advice, not a diagnosis, and not birth control.

Credits

No third party libraries. BLAKE2b and Argon2id are written out in this file and checked against published test vectors by the self test. Sealing uses the browser's own Web Crypto (AES-256-GCM, HKDF-SHA256). Type is set in local system faces. The icon and the dial are drawn in SVG in this file.
